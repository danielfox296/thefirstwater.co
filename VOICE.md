# VOICE.md — Firstwater Copy Voice
*A drafting guide for copy written on Daniel's behalf: site, event pages, ads,
clips, email. Its job is keeping agent drafts on-voice. It does not govern
Daniel; his call always wins, and nothing here restricts what he chooses to
say or do. Last updated 2026-07-22. Reconciled with the 2026-07-21 personal
reframe.*

*Scope: this doc governs the service's transactional emails and
attendee-facing pages (checkout, ticket, intake) with full force — they are
Firstwater's voice.*

## Register
Plain-ceremonial. Ceremony over explanation: the copy enacts the session, it
does not explain the mechanism. Say less. No borrowed shamanism
(chakra/frequency lore, costume language). No clinical or whitepaper voice.
The blog may go deeper than event copy, but it obeys every law below.

The site speaks as Daniel — first person singular ("I", "Write to me"). Two
exceptions: the corporate page (institutional we, deliberate) and the
ratified entity block (communal we, verbatim).

"Never explain the evening" stands. The FAQ and the home "night, plainly"
block are the single sanctioned plain description of the night; positioning
copy everywhere else enacts, never explains.

## Naming law (2026-07-21 reframe)
Daniel, also known as Firstwater. Never "D. Fox" (retired 2026-07-21), never
a faceless brand persona, never third-person self-reference ("the producer
behind").

## Vocabulary law
A session is never a "room." The physical venue is a "space"; the gathering
is a "session." ("Control room" as a literal studio term survives.)

## The agent rule (deepest law)
The reader and the facilitator are the only agents in any sentence.
Abstractions never take verbs. "Your body" and "your mind" are not characters.
Sound may act only as literal physics (pressure in a chest), never as a guide
with intentions.
- BAD:  Sound arrives in the body before the mind can argue.
- GOOD: You cannot talk your way out of something you feel in your chest.
- BAD:  The sound takes you down, then brings you back up.
- GOOD: You drop further than you can get on your own. You come back up.
- BAD:  This room works on the other part of you.
- GOOD: In a session you don't have to talk at all.

## The AI-compression kill list
Run every draft against these before it ships:
1. Conceptual/inanimate agency (see agent rule above)
2. Fragment stacks — "Lights low. No words. Just sound."
3. Anaphora — "Another month of X. Another season of Y."
4. Aphorism twists — "It just gets quieter about how it runs you."
5. Negation pairs — "It wasn't talking, and it wasn't time."
6. Compressed it's-not-X-it's-Y turns — "Most rooms sound cheap. This one
   doesn't."
7. Em dashes — never in site copy.
The fix, always: longer sentences that breathe, real pronouns doing real
actions, mid-thought self-correction ("and if you're honest," "I think that's
because," "whether you asked to learn it or not").

## No apology
Trust adults. Copy never apologizes, discloses defensively, or pre-defends
against "this might be weird." "Not therapy" NEVER appears in copy, including
structured data. Consent lives in the architecture and in one plain
description of the night. Waiver and resource card are door objects.
Confidence is the reassurance.

## Claims frame
Orientation, not law. Facilitated processes toward desired outcomes; the
moving-through belongs to the participant. After-states read truest painted
as possibility and attributed (testimonials, "most people," "some leave
without...").
- Words that fit the register: rest, release, put something down, a space to
  process (internal framing), carry / carrying, lighter.
- Words that usually fight it: heal/treat/cure in a medical sense, fix you,
  transform, journey (as noun for the session), vibration/frequency lore,
  "hold space."
Nothing is out of bounds for Daniel. Drafts default to the register above;
he departs from it whenever it serves.

## Killed lines (2026-07-21 reframe — do not reintroduce, no variants)
- "A dark room. One light. Sound you feel before you hear."
- "Sound you feel before you hear it. A feeling you leave knowing how to
  find." (the retired footer tagline)
- "You feel it in your chest before you hear it."
- The entire "you feel it before you hear/explain it" construction family.
  Agents keep reinventing these; they are dead, not paraphrasable.
- The "How loud does it get?" FAQ answer ("strong in the chest and easy on
  the ear...") — rejected by Daniel. Do not answer volume questions with
  reassurance mechanics; if volume copy is ever needed, Daniel writes it.

## Structure for selling pages
Direct-response architecture under ceremony diction: hook in the reader's
interior language → her situation and its trajectory → the turn (two
sentences of mechanism, no more) → the night, described plainly, once →
after-state as attributed possibility → logistics → ticket-back line.
Volume stays low; the architecture does the selling.

A metaphor is a metaphor. Metaphysical language may appear in the blog

claims stated as fact, not unproven language held as metaphor.

are honest material: for some a window, for some a nap, for some deeper —

## General copy rules imported from the founder voice (foundroom skill)
Adopted for all venture copy; they extend the kill list. Where they collide
with this doc, the resolution is stated inline and this doc wins.

### Additional banned constructions
- The word "quietly."
- Defining scope by negation. Never clarify what something isn't; state what
  it is and let the boundary be inferred. (This generalizes the existing
  "no apology" law — "'Not therapy' never appears" is one instance of it.)
- Vague passive subjects: "what shows up," "what emerges," "what appears."
  Name who notices, who acts, who changes.
- Punchy assertions before context. Earn the claim; walk the reader in first.
  A one-line declaration only works after the orienting is done.
- Labels before mechanisms. Describe the thing, let the reader recognize it,
  then name it. A label introduced early is jargon.
- Consultant/pseudo-engineering diction: load, system, optimize, framework,
  leverage, feedback loop, function, outputs, internal pressure.
- Therapy/coaching diction: healing, processing, integration, safety,
  regulation, trauma, normalize, validate, motivational framing of any kind.
  OVERRIDE: "a space to process" survives as approved internal framing per
  the Claims frame above; it is the single sanctioned exception.
- Emotion-as-weight metaphors ("carries weight," "feels heavy").
  OVERRIDE: "carry / carrying, lighter" remain approved claims words above —
  the ban applies to weight-as-emotional-commentary, not to the literal
  put-something-down vocabulary this venture is built on.

### Additional required behaviors
- Word preferences: "probably" over "likely," "might" over "may"; hedges
  (maybe, often, tends to) when describing possible attendee experience —
  recognition without asserted certainty. This enforces the Claims frame.
- Mechanics over evaluation: no praise, no judgment, cause and effect. When
  tempted to evaluate the reader or her situation, describe the mechanism.
- No reader management: no comprehension scaffolding, no anticipating or
  soothing her reaction, no summarizing implications she can infer. Trust
  adults (same law as "No apology," stated as behavior).
- Rhythm: vary sentence length deliberately; never setup → explanation →
  soft landing. When tempted to shorten a sentence to avoid risk, stay in it
  and add specificity instead.
- The hard stop: end on a concrete behavior or noun and stop. If the last
  sentence could be followed by "and that matters," cut "and that matters."
  This is the founder-voice version of ceremony over explanation, and it is
  the preferred ending for every selling page and blog piece.

### Litmus (run with the kill list before shipping)
1. Does the paragraph describe a mechanism, pattern, or cost?
2. Is any sentence doing interpretive or emotional labor for the reader?
   If yes, delete it.
3. Could the sentence appear in a therapy book or wellness article?
   If yes, it does not belong here.
